BlueSky

Bluesky is a self-help mobile application designed to offer insightful questions to promote judgement-free thinking and reflection about our intentions. With BlueSky, we reach a new level of awareness and mindfulness of our everday actions - blowing away the clouds of obscurity and bringing about a clear, blue sky.



The Challenge

In today's fast paced world, our thoughts, intentions and goals are often clouded . We find ourselves focusing solely on the end goal , without thinking much about the process of the goal . This can result to increased stress levels as we become unsure or even confused about why we are so focused on a certain goal or intention.

The Solution

BlueSky helps people clarify their thoughts and intentions by asking the user introspective questions about their intention. This promotes critical thinking and reflection and helps the user deeply think about the process of their 'intention', rather than simply focusing on the end goal. Ultimately, BlueSky helps the users solidify and achieve their goals through their own mental strength .

Needfinding

We interviewed and observed students and friends about the process of setting a goal or intention and different successes and failures of intention recording with traditional methods . We identified that users need a way to record and reflect upon intentions and need a system that promotes critical thinking of said intentions .

Storyboard

We created several storyboards to assess different user scenarios. One of our storyboards focused on maintaining user engagement while completing tasks , and the other was a 'to-do' list . Over the few weeks, we shifted our emphasis on completing a task to understanding the motivations for completing a task . We realized this would help the user really dig deeper into understanding why they wanted to complete a task and would help them complete it more efficiently .

Prototyping

During needfinding, we discovered that many of the user needs revolved around the inability to remain focused and manage time . Our intial solutions involved using a 'peer-system' that would hold peers accountable for each other's focus . We also envisioned using organizational tools to reduce confusion and increase structure.

User Testing

We tested 31 undergraduates between the ages of 18-25 . We conducted an A/B test with two versions of the application. One version had a 'help' button which clarified the purpose behind the application , whereas the other version did not .

From user testing we found that:

  • Users were confused by what we meant by 'intention' in the first place in the first design . In the second design , the users were able to understand the purpose of the app by reading the brief help documentation.
  • In the first design , users were confused by what the 'send' button did and where the questions were sent . In the second version , we changed the terminology to 'submit' which elimated any confusion .

    • To view more details about the A/B testing, click here!

    Aesthetics, Branding & Final Prototype

    BlueSky comes from the metaphor of the mind as the sky , which is best experienced without 'weather events' such as storms or clouds caused by stress and anxiety . We believe that people are so focused on the end goal that they don't take the time to think before acting, resulting in bad decisions.

    In the color redesign, we used blue because it is known to be a calming color . On the 'intentions' page , we added an animation of clouds to clearly reprise the metaphor.

    CrowdClass

    CrowdClass is a socially translucent, collaborative communication platform that encourages social interaction as well as content exchange



    The Challenge

    In small discussion-type classes that do not have any midterms or exams , students are engaged during the course in inspirational content and are coming up with lots of new ideas and questions. They often take notes haphazardly and lose their notes later . Students in smaller classes need a way to record their contributions automatically while they are talking in discussion. They often don’t write down others’ ideas due to inconvenience or speed , and thus lose the inspiration over time . Thus, they leave the classroom with only their ideas in mind and therefore do not get the entire benefits of the fruitful discussion .

    The Goal

    To design a collaborative, socially translucent, communication environment that facilitates both sharing of information and starting of social relationships during small classroom discussion based classes. By facilitating peer to peer interactions and also peer to group interactions , we hope to create better social relationships between students and faculty, a more collaborative academic environment, and more engagement in classes.

    User Interviews

    We interviewed 20 students between the ages of 18-25. All of these students attended a lecture in a big classroom with more than 100 students as well as lectures in small classrooms with 15-20 students. We asked them questions regarding their experiences in these two different lecture styles.

  • Students in small lectures are less likely to take notes as these classes do not have exams and are more 'discussion based'.
  • Students often lose inspiration to contribute to the group discussion unless there is discussion points.
  • Shy students are hesitant to contribute to the discussion as the classroom.
  • Students do not remember the poitns made by their peers in discussion and thus do not obtain the whole value behind the discussion.

    • Competitive Analysis

    Before we began designing our prototype we first made sure to do a competitive analysis to see what products were already out there and what features they had.

    From our competitive analysis, we found that there was no product that included all the features that we wanted to incorporate into our prototype. Features unique to our prototype are 'Version Control' , Upvoting System , and 'Voice Input' .

    Initial Prototype

    Some functions unique to our prototype are two-way communication and collaboration between the students and teacher, voice recording, real name system and traces from the physical world which included cursor location to be a social cue.

    Final Prototype

    After our various stages of user testing we found out that the voice recognition feature did not work well. We removed that feature in our final prototype and designed a wireframe that incorporated all the features that worked well. The final prototype included features such as public and invidual notes section , questions answered in real-time by the Teaching staff, fully public editing (to encourage collaboration offline), automatic attendance monitoring through participation and peer to peer messaging .

    SmartLot

    SmartLot is a tool that makes finding parking in an open parking area easier, efficient and more convienent.


    The Challenge

    Many​ ​UC San Diego ​students​, employees and staff ​live​ ​off​-campus and commute to campus on a daily basis. ​ ​However,​ ​many​ ​of​ ​the​ ​student​ ​drivers, particularly UC San Diego undergraduate students, find​ ​it​ ​extremely​ ​difficult​ ​to​ ​find​ ​parking​ ​on​ ​campus​ . These students, employees and staff ​ waste​ ​a​ ​lot​ ​of​ ​their​ ​valuable​ ​gas​ ​and time in search for parking. Solving this parking crisis will reduce the stress the commuters face , as​ ​well​ ​as​ ​ help our environment by combating harmful emissions that are produced by the cars whilst circling around campus looking for parking.​

    The Goal

    To come up with a prototype for a smooth-sailing parking experience for commuters which entails that they are easily able to find parking , do not spend more than 10-15 minutes to find parking and informed about the current capacity of the parking lot .

    User Research

    We​ ​used​ ​ Google​ ​Surveys,​ ​conducted​ ​interviews​ ​and​ ​observed​ ​the​ ​ physical design​ ​site​ of the parking lots ​to​ ​gather​ ​information.​ ​​ ​We​ ​posted​ ​the​ ​link​ ​to​ ​the​ ​Google​ ​Surveys​ ​on​ ​various​ ​UCSD​ ​Facebook groups​ ​so​ ​that​ ​they​ ​were​ ​accessible​ ​to​ ​a​ ​wide​ ​range​ ​of​ ​UCSD​ ​students.​ ​​ We​ ​interviewed​ ​between​ ​ 18-24 UCSD​ ​undergraduates​ ​regarding​ ​their​ ​concerns​ ​about​ ​the​ ​parking​ ​situation​ ​on​ ​campus.​ ​​ ​We​ ​also​ ​ observed Muir​ ​Parking​ ​Lot​ ​to​ ​see​ ​if​ ​we​ ​could​ ​find​ ​any​ ​ trends​ ​regarding​ ​when​ ​the​ ​parking​ ​lot​ ​was​ ​the​ ​busiest .​ ​​ ​We visited​ ​Muir​ ​Parking​ ​Lot​ ​at​ ​three​ ​different​ ​times​ ​:​ ​9am,​ ​12pm​ ​and​ ​3pm.​ ​​ ​We​ ​noticed​ ​that​ ​the​ ​parking​ ​lot was​ ​the​ ​ busiest​ ​around​ ​12pm.

    User Problems

    After our user interviews, we found out the main problems the student drivers had were as follows:
    1. Limited amount of S parking spots , which are the spots designated for undergraduate students.
    2. Spending more than 20-30 minutes looking for parking by circling around the parking lots.
    3. Not knowing whether the outdoor parking lots have any available spots .

    Ideation & Design Story


    Prototype 1

    Spiral Parking Lot

    We designed a spiral parking lot structure , which would be replace the Muir Parking lot. Our main goal here was to increase the number of available S spots . By having multiple levels in this parking lot, we predicted that we could increase the number of S parking spots .

    However, after some contemplation and further user testing, we realized this solution had already been implemented on our campus and did not alleviate the parking crisis that the students currently face.


    Prototype 2

    Balloon Indicator

    We wanted a way for the drivers to see where the empty parking spots were in an open parking lot. We came up with the idea of a balloon being a placeholder for the empty parking spots , and when the car parks in that spot, the balloon lowers down , indicating that spot has now been taken.

    However, we decided not to move forward with this prototype because we encountered many issues regarding the implementation of this prototype for user testing.


    Prototype 3

    Sign Redesign

    We designed a simple prototype that would indicate if the parking lot was full or if there were empty spaces. This would save the drivers a lot of time , as they would not even have to enter a parking lot if they knew that there were no available spaces.
    However, after testing this prototype we realized that even if we used these signs, drivers would still enter the parking lot, in hopes of finding a parking space by chance.

    Final Prototype

    For our final prototype, we wanted to combine the ideas we had while ideating and use suggestions from the users. Our​ ​design​ ​solution​ ​is​ ​a​ ​24”​ ​by​ ​36”​ ​white​ ​sign​ ​with​ ​three​ ​dynamic​ ​electronic​ ​numbers​ ​to​ ​depict​ ​the parking​ ​availability .​ ​The​ ​ color​ ​of​ ​the​ ​numbers​ ​will​ ​either​ ​be​ ​green​ ​to​ ​show​ ​the​ ​drivers​ ​there​ ​is​ ​space,​ ​or​ ​it will​ ​be​ ​red​ ​signifying​ ​there​ ​is​ ​no​ ​space .​ ​The​ ​ color​ ​selection​ ​against​ ​a​ ​white​ ​background​ ​creates​ ​salience and​ ​affords​ ​visibility​ ​from​ ​a​ ​far​ ​distance .​ ​Furthermore,​ ​the​ ​colors​ ​build​ ​on​ ​top​ ​of​ ​the​ ​existing​ ​conceptual model​ ​of​ ​drivers​ ​who​ ​associate​ ​green​ ​with​ ​go​ ​and​ ​red​ ​with​ ​stop.​

    Interoceptive Box

    The interoceptive box is an equipment that enables us to study empathy and prosocial behavior in rats.


    The Challenge

    How do you design systems that are going to be used by non-human users? What behaviors of rats must we consider in order to create a model that rats will be able to use? How can we interpret the results from the study and use it to study empathy?

    The Goal

    To design a tool that can be efficiently used by rats and that can help us in studying the biological roots underlying empathy and prosocial behavior in rats.

    The 'iBox'/ Interoceptive Box


    Prototype 1

    This prototype was built using Python and Arduino.

    Testing Double Chambered Apparatus

    The Active rat was able to discern the passive rat's distress. The Active rat was able to correctly press the lever to stop the airflow for the passive rat, as well as for himself.

    We encountered some potential confounds with this study. Did this study really test for empathy and prosocial behavior? What if the active rat simply became trained to press a nose port no matter what? This could indicate that the active rat was not empathizing with the other rat and acting in a prosocial manner.

    Prototype 2

    To address the potential confound, we introduced a third chamber , which was an empty chamber and had corresponding nose ports with it. We hoped to see that the active rat would not press the nose port when the air flow was in the empty chamber.

    Future Work

    The rats will be implanted with electrodes in the insular cortex (this is where interoceptive information is recorded) and amygdala. Flexible electrodes will be implanted on the chest muscle to obtain heart and respiration rate.
